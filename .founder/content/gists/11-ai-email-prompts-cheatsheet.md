## The universal email-prompt formula

Every email prompt worth reusing has six slots. Fill them in order:

```
[ROLE]: who the AI is writing as (e.g. "senior account manager")
[RECIPIENT]: who reads it (their role, seniority, relationship to you)
[CONTEXT]: the thread or situation so far (what they said, what you need)
[GOAL]: what success looks like (reply yes, move deadline, close door politely)
[TONE]: direct / warm / formal / playful (pick ONE)
[CONSTRAINTS]: length, what to avoid, what to include
```

Skip any slot and the model fills it with filler — "I hope this email finds you well," "circling back," "just wanted to check in." The exact phrases your recipient is trained to skim past.

## 1. Polite "no thanks" reply

```
You are me replying to a cold pitch email. I'm not buying, but I don't want to
burn the bridge (they could be useful later, or a mutual connection).

Their pitch:
"[PASTE THEIR EMAIL]"

Write a 3-sentence reply that:
- Declines clearly (no "maybe later" if I mean no)
- Names one specific reason (wrong timing, wrong fit, already solved)
- Leaves one honest door open (a topic, a future trigger, a referral)

No "hope this finds you well". No "thanks for reaching out!". Direct human voice.
```

## 2. Complaint de-escalator

```
You are a senior CX manager. A customer wrote an angry email. Write my reply.

Their message:
"[PASTE COMPLAINT]"

Constraints:
- Acknowledge the specific thing that went wrong in sentence 1 (not "your concerns")
- Take ownership in sentence 2 (we / I, not "the team")
- Offer one concrete fix with a date in sentence 3
- Invite a 10-minute call if the fix isn't enough
- Max 90 words. No apologies stacked 3-deep. No corporate passive voice.

Match their tone one notch calmer — if they're hot, you're room temperature.
```

## 3. Bad-news reply to your boss

```
You are me. I need to tell my manager that [WHAT WENT WRONG]. Reply in 4 sentences:

1. State the news factually, top line first (no wind-up).
2. Quantify the impact (dollars, users, timeline).
3. Name the cause honestly (one sentence, no finger-pointing).
4. Propose one concrete next step and ask for their sign-off.

Tone: calm, owning it, forward-looking. Not "I'm so sorry, I feel terrible."
The goal is for them to trust my judgment more after reading this, not less.
```

## 4. Meeting decline (with a path forward)

```
Write a reply declining this meeting and proposing we handle it async.

Their invite:
"[PASTE]"

Structure:
- Sentence 1: decline the meeting, give one reason (conflict, scope, async better)
- Sentence 2: propose the exact async swap — a doc, a Loom, 3 questions over email
- Sentence 3: specify turnaround ("by Thursday EOD")

Keep it under 70 words. Don't apologize for setting the boundary.
```

## 5. Counter-offer reply

```
They offered [THEIR OFFER]. I want to land at [MY TARGET]. Write my reply.

Context of the deal: [BRIEF CONTEXT — what the work is, our relationship]

Structure:
- Acknowledge their offer concretely (show you read it)
- State my counter clearly with the specific number
- Give ONE reason rooted in value, not need (what they get, not what I need)
- End with a next step (a call, a signed SOW, a start date)

Tone: confident, collaborative, not apologetic. Max 6 sentences.
```

## 6. Gentle follow-up ("bumping this")

```
I sent the email below [N] days ago. No reply yet. Write a 2-sentence bump.

Original email:
"[PASTE]"

Rules:
- Sentence 1: reference the specific ask, not "just checking in"
- Sentence 2: offer ONE way to make replying easier (a yes/no, a time option, a smaller ask)
- Reply-top format: put the new text above the quoted original
- No guilt. No "I know you're busy." No "last chance."
```

## 7. Re-engage dead thread ("circling back" done right)

```
This thread has been silent for [N] days. My goal is ONE honest attempt to restart it
before I close the loop. Write a short email.

Thread summary: [ONE-LINE CONTEXT]
What changed since last reply: [NEW DATA / NEW OFFER / NEW DEADLINE]

Structure:
- Open with the thing that changed (news hook, not "following up")
- Restate the specific ask in 1 sentence
- Give them an easy out: "If this isn't the right time, reply 'later' and I'll close the loop."

Max 5 sentences. Subject line prefix: "Re: " — keep the original subject.
```

## 8. Warm intro request

```
Write an email asking [MUTUAL CONNECTION] to intro me to [TARGET PERSON].

Why I want the intro: [ONE SPECIFIC REASON — not "to network"]
What's in it for the target: [WHAT I BRING — a resource, a buyer, a specific help]

Structure:
- Make the ask forward-able: write two paragraphs that [MUTUAL] can literally paste
  to [TARGET] after a one-line "Hi, want to introduce you two."
- Paragraph 1: who I am + the specific reason I want to meet
- Paragraph 2: what [TARGET] gets out of 20 minutes
- At the top, write one sentence to [MUTUAL] asking if they're willing to forward.

Zero fluff. Zero "if you think it's appropriate." Forward-friendly is the whole point.
```

## 9. Out-of-office + delegation

```
Write my OOO auto-reply for [DATES] while I'm [REASON — optional, can omit].

Coverage:
- [COLLEAGUE NAME] handles [DOMAIN] — their email
- Urgent [SPECIFIC CATEGORY] → [PHONE / SLACK / ESCALATION PATH]
- Everything else: I'll reply on [RETURN DATE]

Constraints:
- Max 5 lines.
- No "sorry for the inconvenience."
- No "checking email periodically" (if I am, don't say it; if I'm not, don't promise it).
- End with a concrete return date, not "soon."
```

## 10. Price objection response

```
A prospect replied: "It's too expensive."
Write my response to keep the deal alive without discounting first.

Deal context: [PRODUCT, PRICE, WHAT IT REPLACES FOR THEM]

Structure:
- Acknowledge the price honestly (don't minimize)
- Reframe around the specific outcome they'd get (dollars saved, hours back, risk removed)
- Ask ONE diagnostic question: "Expensive vs. [WHAT]?" — budget, ROI, alternative, now-vs-later
- Offer ONE path forward that isn't a discount (smaller scope, phased start, trial)

Max 6 sentences. Warm, confident. No "I totally understand" — that's the smell of a discount.
```

---

## 11. Deadline renegotiation

```
I can't hit the [ORIGINAL DATE] deadline. I need to move it to [NEW DATE].

Context: [WHY — one honest sentence, no over-explaining]

Structure:
- Sentence 1: state the new date top line, no wind-up
- Sentence 2: one honest cause (don't blame; don't over-explain)
- Sentence 3: what's still on track (what they'll get on time)
- Sentence 4: one de-risk I'm adding so this doesn't slip again
- Sentence 5: ask for their explicit yes / push-back

Tone: calm, owning the slip, pragmatic. Not "I'm so sorry" six times.
```

## 12. Apology email (no grovel)

```
I [WHAT I DID — the screw-up, concretely]. Write my apology.

Impact on them: [THEIR CONCRETE IMPACT]

Rules:
- Name the specific thing (not "recent issues")
- Own it in the active voice: "I missed" not "the deliverable was missed"
- Skip reasons/excuses — state the cause ONLY if it helps them trust the fix
- State the specific fix + date
- Close with one line: "What else do you need from me?"

Max 90 words. Apologize once. Don't apologize for apologizing. Don't promise perfection.
```

## 13. Long-thread summarizer

```
You are my chief of staff. Summarize the email thread below for me.

Thread:
"[PASTE FULL THREAD]"

Return:
1. **The ask** (one sentence — what's the decision or action someone needs)
2. **State of play** (3 bullets — where each stakeholder landed)
3. **Open questions** (bullets — things nobody's answered yet)
4. **My move** (one sentence — what the sender of the last email is waiting for from me)

Be brutal about what matters. Strip social niceties. Flag any deadline.
```

## 14. Rambling-draft tightener

```
Tighten the draft below. Target: half the words, same message.

My draft:
"[PASTE 300-WORD DRAFT]"

Rules:
- Cut every "I just wanted to", "I hope you're well", "quick question"
- Replace passive voice with active
- Lead with the ask — move it to sentence 1 if it's buried
- Keep any specific numbers, names, dates verbatim
- Max 120 words out. If you can't hit 120, tell me which sentence is load-bearing and why.
```

## Model comparison — when to use what

| Model           | Best for                                    | Watch-out                                           |
|-----------------|---------------------------------------------|-----------------------------------------------------|
| **GPT-4o / 4.1**| Fast replies, tone-matching, short threads  | Can over-soften — add "direct" to your tone slot    |
| **Claude 3.5+** | Nuanced threads, de-escalation, apologies   | Verbose by default — set hard word caps             |
| **Gemini 2+**   | Long-thread summaries, multi-quote context  | Weaker on tone — give 1 example of voice you want   |
| **Llama 3+**    | Private inbox data, local on Ollama         | Weaker at subtlety — pair with stricter constraints |

## Common mistakes

1. **"Hope this finds you well"** — flagged instantly as template mail. Cut every opener that doesn't carry information.
2. **Burying the ask in paragraph 3.** Lead with the ask; context after. If the recipient only reads sentence 1, they should know what you need.
3. **Stacked apologies.** One "sorry" per email. More than that reads as either panic or theater.
4. **Passive voice on bad news.** "The deadline was missed" protects no one and costs trust. Use "I missed".
5. **"Circling back" with no new information.** If nothing has changed since your last email, you don't need to send one. Wait until something changes.
