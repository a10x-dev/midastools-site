# Midas Tools — Freelancer AI Kit
# 03: Client Onboarding System

First impressions set the tone for the entire engagement. These templates help you onboard new clients with confidence and professionalism.

---

## Variables Reference

```
[YOUR NAME] — Your name or business name
[YOUR COMPANY] — Your business name
[YOUR EMAIL] — Your contact email
[YOUR CALENDAR LINK] — Scheduling link (Calendly, etc.)
[CLIENT NAME] — Client's first name
[CLIENT COMPANY] — Client's business name
[PROJECT NAME] — Name of the project
[PROJECT TYPE] — What you're delivering
[START DATE] — Project start date
[END DATE] — Expected completion date
[DELIVERABLES] — Key outputs
[COMMUNICATION TOOL] — e.g., "Slack", "Email", "Basecamp"
[PROJECT MANAGEMENT TOOL] — e.g., "Notion", "Trello", "Asana"
[MEETING CADENCE] — e.g., "Weekly on Tuesdays at 2pm ET"
[RESPONSE TIME] — e.g., "Within 24 business hours"
[REVISION ROUNDS] — Number of revision rounds included
[PAYMENT SCHEDULE] — When payments are due
```

## Prompt 1: Welcome Email — Sent Immediately After Contract Signing

### Prompt

```
Write a welcome email to send to a new freelance client immediately after they've signed the contract and made their initial payment.

CONTEXT:
- My name: [YOUR NAME] at [YOUR COMPANY]
- Client: [CLIENT NAME] at [CLIENT COMPANY]
- Project: [PROJECT NAME] — [PROJECT TYPE]
- Start date: [START DATE]
- First milestone: [Describe the first major deliverable or milestone]

Write an email that:
- Has an upbeat, professional subject line (e.g., "We're officially underway — here's what happens next")
- Congratulates them on taking action / investing in [what the project will achieve]
- Confirms the project start date
- Previews the first 7 days — what you'll be doing, what they should expect to hear from you
- Lists 2-3 things you need from them to get started (keep it small and specific)
- Tells them exactly when they'll hear from you next
- Includes your preferred contact method and response time commitment
- Closes with genuine enthusiasm for the project

TONE: Energetic and professional. They just made a buying decision — reinforce that it was the right one. Make them feel like they're in great hands.

LENGTH: Under 200 words.
```

### How to Use
Send this within 1 hour of receiving the signed contract. Speed signals professionalism. Attach or link any relevant documents (contract copy, intake form, etc.).

## Prompt 2: Welcome Email — Project Kickoff Details (Day 2-3)

### Prompt

```
Write a detailed project kickoff email to send 2-3 days after the welcome email.

CONTEXT:
- My name: [YOUR NAME] at [YOUR COMPANY]
- Client: [CLIENT NAME] at [CLIENT COMPANY]
- Project: [PROJECT NAME]
- Project type: [PROJECT TYPE]
- Start date: [START DATE]
- End date: [END DATE]
- Key deliverables: [DELIVERABLES]
- Communication tool: [COMMUNICATION TOOL]
- Project management tool: [PROJECT MANAGEMENT TOOL]
- Meeting cadence: [MEETING CADENCE]
- My response time: [RESPONSE TIME]
- Revision rounds included: [REVISION ROUNDS]

Write an email that covers:

1. **Project Overview** — Brief restatement of what we're building and why
2. **Timeline Snapshot** — Key milestones and dates (3-5 bullet points)
3. **How We'll Work Together:**
   - Communication: Where and how to reach me
   - Meetings: Recurring schedule, format, and duration
   - Updates: How and when I'll share progress
   - Feedback: How to give feedback and what makes a revision round
4. **Your Action Items** — Numbered list of 3-5 things the client needs to provide or complete before the kickoff meeting (content, access credentials, brand assets, etc.)
5. **Kickoff Meeting** — Propose a date/time or share a calendar link
6. **Questions?** — Invite them to ask anything before the kickoff

TONE: Organized and reassuring. Show them you have a process. This email should make them think "I'm in good hands."

LENGTH: 250-350 words.
```

### How to Use
This is your most important onboarding email. It sets expectations for the entire project. Customize the action items based on what you actually need from this specific client.

## Prompt 3: Welcome Email — Week 1 Check-In (Day 5-7)

### Prompt

```
Write a brief check-in email to send at the end of the first week of a new freelance project.

CONTEXT:
- My name: [YOUR NAME]
- Client: [CLIENT NAME]
- Project: [PROJECT NAME]
- What I've accomplished this week: [2-3 bullet points of progress]
- Any blockers or items I'm waiting on from the client: [List or "None"]
- Next week's plan: [2-3 bullet points]

Write an email that:
- Has a concise subject line (e.g., "Week 1 Update — [PROJECT NAME]")
- Briefly recaps what was accomplished
- Flags any blockers or outstanding items (diplomatically)
- Previews what's coming next week
- Asks if they have any questions or concerns
- Takes under 60 seconds to read

TONE: Confident and proactive. You're running the project — this is a status report, not a request for direction.

LENGTH: Under 150 words.
```

### How to Use
This closes the onboarding loop. After this, transition to your regular update cadence (see `06-weekly-client-updates.md`).

## Prompt 4: Project Kickoff Questionnaire Generator

### Prompt

```
Generate a project kickoff questionnaire for a new freelance client.

CONTEXT:
- Project type: [PROJECT TYPE]
- Client industry: [CLIENT INDUSTRY]
- Client: [CLIENT NAME] at [CLIENT COMPANY]

Create a questionnaire with 15-25 questions organized into these sections:

**1. Business & Brand**
- Questions about their company, target audience, brand voice, competitors
- Adapt to [CLIENT INDUSTRY]

**2. Project Goals**
- What does success look like?
- What problem are we solving?
- How will they measure results?
- Are there existing benchmarks or data?

**3. Scope & Preferences**
- Specific requirements, must-haves, and nice-to-haves
- Design/style preferences (if applicable)
- Examples of work they admire (competitors or otherwise)
- Things they definitely do NOT want

**4. Content & Assets**
- What content/materials do they already have?
- What needs to be created?
- Who is responsible for providing content?
- Brand guidelines, logos, fonts, color codes?

**5. Technical Requirements** (if applicable)
- Existing tools, platforms, or integrations
- Login credentials or access needed
- Technical constraints or requirements

**6. Logistics**
- Who is the primary point of contact?
- Who else needs to review or approve work?
- Decision-making process (one approver or committee?)
- Preferred communication channel and frequency

FORMAT: Numbered questions, grouped by section. Include a brief instruction paragraph at the top explaining the purpose and estimated completion time (15-20 minutes).

Add a note at the bottom: "Don't worry if you don't have answers to everything — we'll work through it together during our kickoff call."
```

### How to Use
Send this before the kickoff meeting. Review their answers beforehand so you come to the meeting prepared with follow-up questions.

## Prompt 5: Expectations-Setting Document

### Prompt

```
Generate a professional "Working Together" document that sets expectations for a freelance engagement.

CONTEXT:
- My name: [YOUR NAME] at [YOUR COMPANY]
- Client: [CLIENT NAME] at [CLIENT COMPANY]
- Project: [PROJECT NAME]
- Timeline: [START DATE] to [END DATE]
- Communication tool: [COMMUNICATION TOOL]
- Meeting cadence: [MEETING CADENCE]
- Response time: [RESPONSE TIME]
- Revision rounds: [REVISION ROUNDS]

Create a document with these sections:

**Communication**
- Primary channel: [COMMUNICATION TOOL]
- Response times: What they can expect from me and what I expect from them
- Business hours: [Your working hours and timezone]
- Urgent requests: How to handle them and what qualifies as urgent

**Meetings**
- Recurring meeting: [MEETING CADENCE]
- Meeting format: agenda sent 24 hours in advance, notes sent after
- Cancellation/reschedule policy

**Feedback & Revisions**
- How to give feedback (specific, consolidated, written)
- What counts as a revision round
- Number of rounds included: [REVISION ROUNDS]
- Additional revisions: how they're priced

**Approvals**
- What requires formal approval before proceeding
- How to give approval (email confirmation, sign-off form, etc.)
- Impact of delayed approvals on the timeline

**Deliverables**
- How files will be shared (Google Drive, Dropbox, etc.)
- File formats
- Final delivery process

**Scope Changes**
- How to request changes (written request)
- Impact assessment process
- Approval required before additional work begins

**Billing**
- Payment schedule: [PAYMENT SCHEDULE]
- Invoice delivery method
- Payment terms and late policy

TONE: Clear, collaborative, professional. This isn't a legal document — it's a partnership guide. Use "we" language where appropriate.

FORMAT: Clean headers and bullet points. This should be a 1-2 page document.
```

### How to Use
Share this during onboarding alongside the kickoff questionnaire. Reference it if expectations are ever misaligned during the project.

## Prompt 6: Communication Preferences Template

### Prompt

```
Generate a "Communication Preferences" card that I can share with new clients to establish how we'll communicate.

CONTEXT:
- My name: [YOUR NAME]
- Best way to reach me: [COMMUNICATION TOOL]
- My business hours: [e.g., "Mon-Fri, 9am-5pm ET"]
- My response time commitment: [RESPONSE TIME]
- My calendar link: [YOUR CALENDAR LINK]

Create a concise, visually clean card (text format) that includes:

**How to Reach Me**
- For quick questions: [Channel, e.g., "Slack DM"]
- For detailed requests: [Channel, e.g., "Email"]
- For meetings: [Calendar link]
- For emergencies: [Method, e.g., "Text message to [PHONE]"]

**When I'm Available**
- Business hours and timezone
- When I check and respond to messages
- Weekend/evening availability (or lack thereof)

**What to Expect**
- My response time for messages
- My turnaround time for deliverables
- How I'll communicate delays or blockers

**How to Give Me Feedback**
- Preferred format (written, Loom video, annotated screenshots)
- Where to send it
- How specific to be

Keep it under 150 words. This should be a quick-reference card, not a document.

Also generate a version I can send to the client to fill out about THEIR preferences, with the same categories but as questions:
- "What's the best way to reach you for quick questions?"
- "What are your working hours?"
- Etc.
```

### How to Use
Exchange communication preferences during onboarding. It takes 5 minutes and prevents weeks of miscommunication.

## Prompt 7: Tool & Access Request Checklist Generator

### Prompt

```
Generate a comprehensive access and tool request checklist for a new freelance project.

CONTEXT:
- Project type: [PROJECT TYPE]
- Client: [CLIENT NAME] at [CLIENT COMPANY]
- Tools I'll need access to: [List any known tools, e.g., "WordPress admin, Google Analytics, Figma workspace"]
- Assets I'll need: [List any known assets, e.g., "Brand guidelines, logo files, product photos"]

Create a checklist organized into these categories:

**Platform & Tool Access**
- List every platform, tool, or account I might need access to for [PROJECT TYPE]
- Include login method preferences (dedicated account, SSO, etc.)
- Note which items need admin access vs. view-only

**Existing Assets**
- Brand assets (logo, colors, fonts, style guide)
- Content (copy, images, videos, documents)
- Data (analytics, customer data, research)
- Technical assets (codebase, hosting, domain, DNS)

**Third-Party Accounts**
- Any vendor or service accounts I need to interface with
- API keys or integrations

**People Access**
- Who to contact for technical questions
- Who to contact for content/brand questions
- Who to contact for billing/contract questions

**Security & Cleanup**
- How access should be provisioned (separate account, temporary credentials)
- Reminder to revoke access at project end
- NDA or security requirements

FORMAT: Checkbox format (- [ ] Item). Group by category. Include a "Priority" column noting which items are needed before the kickoff vs. which can wait.

At the top, include a brief instruction paragraph and a requested completion date.
```

### How to Use
Send this immediately after the contract is signed. Getting access early prevents project delays. Follow up on any missing items before the kickoff meeting.

## Prompt 8: Onboarding Sequence Planner

### Prompt

```
Create a complete onboarding sequence plan for a new freelance client.

CONTEXT:
- My name: [YOUR NAME]
- Client: [CLIENT NAME] at [CLIENT COMPANY]
- Project: [PROJECT NAME]
- Contract signed date: [DATE]
- Project start date: [START DATE]
- Kickoff meeting date: [DATE or "TBD"]

Generate a day-by-day onboarding plan from contract signing to the end of Week 1:

| Day | Action | Channel | Owner | Status |
|-----|--------|---------|-------|--------|

Include these actions:
1. Send welcome email (Day 0 — within 1 hour)
2. Send kickoff questionnaire (Day 0)
3. Send access/tool request checklist (Day 0)
4. Send Communication Preferences card (Day 1)
5. Send Working Together document (Day 1)
6. Send kickoff meeting invite (Day 1-2)
7. Follow up on outstanding questionnaire/access items (Day 3)
8. Kickoff meeting (Day 3-5)
9. Send kickoff meeting notes + confirmed action items (same day as meeting)
10. Begin work (Day 5-7)
11. Send Week 1 check-in email (Day 7)

For each action, include:
- What to send (reference the specific template from this kit)
- What channel to use
- Who is responsible (you or the client)
- Any dependencies

After the table, add a "Red Flags" section: 5 warning signs during onboarding that a project may have trouble (e.g., client doesn't respond to the questionnaire, can't identify a single decision-maker, etc.).
```

### How to Use
Use this as your master onboarding checklist. Adapt the timeline to your project's needs. The key principle: front-load communication to prevent problems later.

## Pro Tips

### The Onboarding Window
You have roughly 7 days from contract signing to establish the working rhythm. After that, patterns are set and harder to change. Use this window well.

### Over-Communication Beats Under-Communication
In the first week, err on the side of sending too many updates rather than too few. Clients are nervous after making a purchase — regular communication reassures them.

### Systematize Everything
If you onboard more than 2-3 clients per year, turn this sequence into a template in your project management tool. Duplicate it for each new client and check off items as you go.

### The Kickoff Meeting is Sacred
Never skip the kickoff meeting, even for small projects. Fifteen minutes of face-to-face (or voice) alignment saves hours of back-and-forth later.

### Document Decisions
From Day 1, document every decision in writing. "As discussed on our call today, we'll go with Option B for the homepage layout." This protects both parties.
