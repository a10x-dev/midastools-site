# Midas Tools — Freelancer AI Kit
# 06: Weekly Client Updates & Communication Templates

Consistent communication is the easiest way to retain clients and get referrals. These templates make your weekly updates, milestone reports, and project wrap-ups effortless.

---

## Variables Reference

```
[YOUR NAME] — Your name or business name
[YOUR COMPANY] — Your business name
[CLIENT NAME] — Client's first name
[CLIENT COMPANY] — Client's business name
[PROJECT NAME] — Name of the project
[WEEK NUMBER] — Current week of the project (e.g., "Week 3")
[TOTAL WEEKS] — Total expected project duration in weeks
[ACCOMPLISHMENTS] — What you completed this week (bullet points)
[BLOCKERS] — Issues or items you're waiting on
[NEXT WEEK PLAN] — What you'll work on next
[MILESTONE NAME] — Name of the milestone achieved
[MILESTONE DELIVERABLES] — What was delivered
[DELAY REASON] — Why the project is delayed
[NEW TIMELINE] — Updated delivery date
[ORIGINAL TIMELINE] — Original delivery date
[DAYS DELAYED] — Number of days of delay
[PROJECT SUMMARY] — Overall project description
[KEY OUTCOMES] — Results or deliverables from the project
[CLIENT FEEDBACK LINK] — Link to a feedback form or survey
[TESTIMONIAL PROMPT] — Specific outcome to reference when asking for a testimonial
```

## Prompt 1: Weekly Progress Report

### Prompt

```
Generate a weekly progress report email for a freelance project.

CONTEXT:
- My name: [YOUR NAME]
- Client: [CLIENT NAME] at [CLIENT COMPANY]
- Project: [PROJECT NAME]
- Week: [WEEK NUMBER] of [TOTAL WEEKS]

THIS WEEK:
Accomplishments:
[ACCOMPLISHMENTS]

Blockers or waiting items:
[BLOCKERS]

NEXT WEEK:
Planned work:
[NEXT WEEK PLAN]

Write an email that includes:

**Subject line:** "[PROJECT NAME] — Week [WEEK NUMBER] Update"

**Body:**
1. **Progress Summary** — 1-2 sentences overview of how the week went
2. **Completed This Week** — Bullet list of accomplishments (use my notes above, but clean them up into client-friendly language)
3. **In Progress** — Any items started but not yet complete
4. **Blockers / Action Items for [CLIENT NAME]** — Anything you need from the client, with specific asks and deadlines. If no blockers, state "No blockers this week."
5. **Up Next** — What I'll focus on in Week [WEEK NUMBER + 1]
6. **Overall Status** — One of: On Track / At Risk / Delayed — with a one-line explanation

TONE: Confident and proactive. This should take the client 60 seconds to read. No fluff, no filler.

LENGTH: Under 200 words.

IMPORTANT:
- Translate technical tasks into business outcomes where possible (e.g., "Built the checkout flow" becomes "Completed the checkout experience — customers can now purchase in 3 clicks")
- If there are blockers, be specific about what you need and by when
- Never hide bad news — address issues directly with a proposed solution
```

### How to Use
Send this every Friday afternoon or Monday morning — pick a day and be consistent. Even during slow weeks, send an update. Silence makes clients nervous.

## Prompt 2: Milestone Completion Email

### Prompt

```
Write a milestone completion email for a freelance project.

CONTEXT:
- My name: [YOUR NAME]
- Client: [CLIENT NAME] at [CLIENT COMPANY]
- Project: [PROJECT NAME]
- Milestone: [MILESTONE NAME]
- Deliverables: [MILESTONE DELIVERABLES]
- What's next: [Next phase or milestone]
- Is client review/approval needed? [Yes/No]
- Review deadline: [Date client should provide feedback by]

Write an email that:
- Has a clear subject line indicating a milestone was reached (e.g., "Milestone Complete: [MILESTONE NAME]")
- Opens with a brief celebration — this is a moment of progress
- Lists exactly what was completed and delivered
- Specifies where to find the deliverables (link, attachment, etc.)
- If review is needed: states what you need them to review, how to provide feedback, and the deadline
- If no review needed: states what comes next and when
- Reminds them of the overall project timeline and where this milestone fits
- Closes with an invitation for questions or a quick call

TONE: Positive and organized. Milestones are moments to reinforce that the project is moving forward and they made the right choice hiring you.

LENGTH: Under 180 words.

Also generate a "Milestone Checklist" — 5 items to verify before sending the milestone email:
- [ ] All deliverables are complete and tested
- [ ] Files are organized and accessible
- [ ] etc.
```

### How to Use
Send milestone emails the same day you complete the milestone. Include clear instructions on what you need from the client and by when. A milestone without a feedback deadline is a milestone that stalls.

## Prompt 3: Project Delay Notification

### Prompt

```
Write a professional project delay notification email to a freelance client.

CONTEXT:
- My name: [YOUR NAME]
- Client: [CLIENT NAME] at [CLIENT COMPANY]
- Project: [PROJECT NAME]
- Original delivery date: [ORIGINAL TIMELINE]
- New delivery date: [NEW TIMELINE]
- Delay: [DAYS DELAYED] days
- Reason for delay: [DELAY REASON]
- Is the delay my fault? [Yes/No]
- What I'm doing to mitigate: [Actions being taken]

Write an email that:
- Has a straightforward subject line (e.g., "Timeline Update: [PROJECT NAME]")
- Opens by stating the situation directly — do not bury the news
- Explains the reason clearly and honestly (no excuses, no jargon)
- States the new delivery date with confidence (do not say "hopefully" or "should be")
- Explains what steps you're taking to get back on track
- Acknowledges the impact on their plans and offers to discuss
- If the delay is your responsibility: take ownership, no deflection
- If the delay is due to external factors or client dependencies: state the facts diplomatically
- Closes with an offer to jump on a quick call if they want to discuss

TONE: Direct, honest, solution-oriented. Clients respect transparency far more than excuses. The worst thing you can do is stay silent when a project is falling behind.

LENGTH: Under 180 words.

Also generate:
- **3 things to NEVER say in a delay notification:**
  1. [Bad phrase] — why it's bad — [better alternative]
  2. ...
  3. ...

- **Delay mitigation options to offer:**
  - Option A: [Adjusted scope to meet original deadline]
  - Option B: [Extended timeline with full scope]
  - Option C: [Phased delivery — deliver critical items on time, remainder later]
```

### How to Use
Send this the moment you know a delay is likely — do not wait until the due date. Early notification builds trust. Late notification destroys it.

## Prompt 4: End-of-Project Wrap-Up Report

### Prompt

```
Generate an end-of-project wrap-up report for a freelance engagement.

CONTEXT:
- My name: [YOUR NAME] at [YOUR COMPANY]
- Client: [CLIENT NAME] at [CLIENT COMPANY]
- Project: [PROJECT NAME]
- Project duration: [START DATE] to [END DATE]
- Original scope: [Brief description]
- Key outcomes: [KEY OUTCOMES]
- Any scope changes during the project: [List or "None"]
- Final deliverables: [List all files, assets, documents, etc.]

Generate a professional wrap-up report with these sections:

**1. Project Summary**
- Brief overview of what was accomplished (2-3 sentences)
- Reference the original goals and confirm they were met

**2. Deliverables**
A complete inventory of everything delivered:
| Deliverable | Description | Format | Location |

**3. Key Outcomes & Metrics**
- Quantifiable results where available
- Before/after comparisons if applicable
- Key improvements or achievements

**4. Scope Changes**
- Summary of any approved changes during the project
- Final project value vs. original estimate

**5. Recommendations**
- 3-5 suggestions for next steps, improvements, or ongoing maintenance
- Quick wins they can implement immediately
- Longer-term opportunities (which you could help with)

**6. Knowledge Transfer**
- Login credentials or access provided
- Documentation locations
- How to maintain or update what was built
- Support contact information

**7. Project Retrospective**
- What went well
- What could be improved for next time
- Lessons learned

**8. Next Steps**
- Any remaining action items
- Warranty/support period details (if applicable)
- How to engage for future work

TONE: Thorough, professional, forward-looking. This document closes the project cleanly and positions you for future work.

LENGTH: 300-400 words total across all sections.
```

### How to Use
Send this within 48 hours of final delivery. A professional wrap-up report sets you apart from 95% of freelancers who just stop communicating after the last deliverable.

## Prompt 5: Testimonial Request Email

### Prompt

```
Write a testimonial request email for a completed freelance project.

CONTEXT:
- My name: [YOUR NAME]
- Client: [CLIENT NAME] at [CLIENT COMPANY]
- Project: [PROJECT NAME]
- Key result: [TESTIMONIAL PROMPT — the specific outcome that would make the best testimonial]
- Where I'll use it: [e.g., "My website and LinkedIn"]
- How long since project ended: [e.g., "1 week", "1 month"]

Write an email that:
- Has a warm, low-pressure subject line
- Opens by referencing the project and a specific positive result
- States that their feedback would mean a lot (genuine, not formulaic)
- Makes it EASY to respond by providing 3 guiding questions:
  1. What was the situation before we started?
  2. What was it like working together?
  3. What results have you seen since the project was completed?
- Offers alternative formats: written paragraph, bullet points, or a 5-minute Loom video
- States where the testimonial will be used and offers to keep it anonymous if preferred
- Notes that 3-5 sentences is perfect — no pressure to write an essay
- Closes gratefully

TONE: Genuine and easy-going. A testimonial is a favor — make it as friction-free as possible.

LENGTH: Under 150 words.

Also generate:
- A **follow-up nudge** (sent 7 days later if no response) — under 50 words
- A **LinkedIn recommendation request message** — under 80 words
- A template for **turning their raw feedback into a polished testimonial** for your website (that you send back for approval)
```

### How to Use
Send the testimonial request 1-2 weeks after project completion — soon enough that the experience is fresh, but not so soon that they haven't seen results. Always ask. Most happy clients are willing; they just need to be prompted.

## Prompt 6: Client Check-In (Post-Project)

### Prompt

```
Write a post-project check-in email to send to a past client.

CONTEXT:
- My name: [YOUR NAME]
- Client: [CLIENT NAME] at [CLIENT COMPANY]
- Project: [PROJECT NAME]
- Completed: [How long ago, e.g., "3 months ago"]
- What I delivered: [Brief summary]
- Something I noticed: [A relevant observation, industry news, or suggestion — e.g., "I noticed your site traffic doubled since we launched" or "There's a new tool that could improve your workflow"]

Write an email that:
- Reconnects warmly without being salesy
- References the specific project and a positive outcome
- Shares the observation or tip (providing value, not asking for anything)
- Casually mentions you're available if they need anything
- Does NOT hard-sell or pitch a new project
- Keeps the door open for future work naturally

TONE: Like a message from a trusted colleague, not a vendor. Genuine, helpful, brief.

LENGTH: Under 120 words.
```

### How to Use
Set a calendar reminder to check in with past clients every 60-90 days. These lightweight touchpoints generate more repeat business than any marketing campaign.

## Prompt 7: "Bad News" Communication Template

### Prompt

```
Write an email delivering difficult project news to a freelance client.

CONTEXT:
- My name: [YOUR NAME]
- Client: [CLIENT NAME] at [CLIENT COMPANY]
- Project: [PROJECT NAME]
- The issue: [Describe — e.g., "Discovered a technical limitation that prevents the original approach", "A key vendor dependency fell through", "My subcontractor missed a deadline"]
- Impact: [How this affects the project — timeline, cost, deliverables]
- My proposed solution: [Your plan to fix or mitigate the issue]
- What I need from the client: [Any decisions or input needed]

Write an email that follows this structure:
1. **State the issue** — Lead with it. No long preamble.
2. **Explain the impact** — What does this mean for the project concretely?
3. **Present the solution** — Show you've already thought this through
4. **Offer options** — Give them choices where possible
5. **Take responsibility** — If it's your fault, own it. One sentence. Don't over-apologize.
6. **Next steps** — What happens now and when they'll hear from you next

RULES:
- Never bury bad news at the end of an email
- Never blame the client, even if it's partly their fault
- Never minimize the issue ("It's no big deal") — take it seriously
- Always pair a problem with a proposed solution
- Be specific about impact (dates, dollars, deliverables)

TONE: Calm, direct, professional. You're reporting a problem and leading the solution, not panicking.

LENGTH: Under 200 words.
```

### How to Use
Delivering bad news well is a rare skill that builds deep trust. Clients remember how you handled problems more than they remember the problems themselves.

## Prompt 8: Monthly Retainer Report

### Prompt

```
Generate a monthly retainer report for an ongoing freelance client.

CONTEXT:
- My name: [YOUR NAME] at [YOUR COMPANY]
- Client: [CLIENT NAME] at [CLIENT COMPANY]
- Retainer month: [e.g., "March 2026"]
- Monthly retainer: [Amount]
- Hours included: [e.g., "20 hours"]
- Hours used this month: [Actual hours]

THIS MONTH'S WORK:
[List all tasks completed with approximate hours per task]

Write a monthly report with these sections:

**1. Summary**
- 2-3 sentence overview of the month
- Overall status: On Track / Scaling Up / Needs Discussion

**2. Hours Breakdown**
| Task | Hours | Notes |
- Include all tasks
- Show total used vs. total included
- Note any rollover or overage

**3. Key Accomplishments**
- 3-5 bullet points of the most impactful work this month
- Translate tasks into business outcomes where possible

**4. Metrics & Results** (if applicable)
- Any measurable outcomes from work performed
- Before/after data where available

**5. Recommendations**
- 2-3 suggestions for next month
- Opportunities you've identified
- Any process improvements

**6. Next Month Preview**
- Planned work for the upcoming month
- Any dates or deadlines to be aware of
- Items that need client input or approval

**7. Retainer Health Check**
- Are the included hours sufficient?
- Is the scope of work well-matched to the retainer level?
- Any recommended adjustments to the retainer terms?

TONE: Results-focused and strategic. This report should reinforce the value of the retainer and make it an easy renewal decision.

FORMAT: Clean, scannable, with clear headers. Under 400 words total.
```

### How to Use
Send retainer reports on the first business day of the following month. Consistent reporting is the number one factor in retainer renewals. If they can see the value, they keep paying.

## Pro Tips

### The Friday Report Habit
Send weekly updates on Friday afternoon. It lets the client go into the weekend knowing the project is handled. It also forces you to reflect on your progress.

### Translate, Don't Transcribe
Clients don't care about technical tasks. They care about progress toward their goals. "Refactored the database queries" means nothing. "Your dashboard now loads 3x faster" means everything.

### The 30-Second Rule
Every client email should be readable in 30 seconds or less. Use headers, bullets, and bold text. If they need more detail, they'll ask.

### Proactive > Reactive
Don't wait for clients to ask for updates. The freelancers who communicate proactively are the ones who get rehired and referred.

### Keep a Communication Log
Track every update you send. If a relationship sours or a dispute arises, your communication history is your best defense.
