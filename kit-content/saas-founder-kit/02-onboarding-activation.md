## Set Your Onboarding Context

Paste this at the start of every AI session before using any prompt below:

```
You are a SaaS onboarding and activation specialist. You understand user psychology, activation metrics, and email sequence design.

Product: [PRODUCT NAME]
What it does: [ONE-SENTENCE DESCRIPTION]
Target user: [PRIMARY PERSONA — role, company size, pain point]
Aha moment: [THE KEY ACTION THAT CORRELATES WITH RETENTION — e.g., "creating their first project", "connecting a data source", "inviting a teammate"]
Trial length: [FREE TRIAL DURATION — e.g., 14 days]
Pricing: [STARTER PLAN PRICE AND WHAT'S INCLUDED]
Key onboarding steps: [LIST THE 3-5 SETUP STEPS A NEW USER NEEDS TO COMPLETE]
Brand voice: [TONE — e.g., friendly, professional, developer-focused, playful]

All emails should be concise, action-oriented, and focused on getting users to value as fast as possible.
```

## Welcome Email Sequence

### Email 1: Welcome Email (Sent Immediately)

```
Write a welcome email for [PRODUCT NAME] sent immediately after signup.

Context:
- User just created their account
- They haven't used the product yet
- First action they should take: [KEY FIRST ACTION — e.g., "create your first workspace", "connect your data source"]
- Quick-start link: [URL TO GETTING STARTED GUIDE OR FIRST ACTION]

Requirements:
- Subject line: 3 options (welcome, action-oriented, benefit-focused)
- From name: [FOUNDER NAME] from [PRODUCT NAME] (personal touch)
- Thank them for signing up in one sentence — don't overdo it
- Immediately direct them to ONE action (not a list of features)
- Include a button CTA linked to the first action
- Add a P.S. line offering to help ("Reply to this email anytime — I read every one")
- Total body: 80-120 words

Do NOT:
- List all features
- Include multiple CTAs
- Use generic "Welcome aboard!" language
- Write more than 3 short paragraphs
```

> **Why this works:** Welcome emails have the highest open rate of any automated email (50-60%). Wasting this attention on a feature list instead of a single clear action is the #1 onboarding mistake SaaS companies make.

### Email 2: Quick Win (Day 1, 4 Hours After Signup)

```
Write a "quick win" email for [PRODUCT NAME] sent 4 hours after signup.

Context:
- User may or may not have logged in yet
- Goal: Get them to complete [FIRST ACTION] if they haven't, or celebrate if they have
- Quick win: [THE SIMPLEST VALUABLE THING THEY CAN DO — should take under 5 minutes]
- Link: [URL TO THE ACTION]

Requirements:
- Subject line: 3 options (curiosity, time-based, achievement)
- Show them exactly what they can accomplish in 5 minutes or less
- Include a 3-step mini-tutorial:
  Step 1: [FIRST STEP]
  Step 2: [SECOND STEP]
  Step 3: [THIRD STEP — the payoff]
- One button CTA
- Optional: Include a screenshot or GIF description of the finished result
- 80-120 words
```

### Email 3: Value Story (Day 2)

```
Write a "value story" email for [PRODUCT NAME] sent on Day 2.

Context:
- Goal: Show them what's possible when they fully adopt the product
- Customer example: [DESCRIBE A REAL OR REPRESENTATIVE CUSTOMER SUCCESS — role, company, result]
- The specific way they use the product: [HOW THEY USE IT]
- The result: [QUANTIFIABLE OUTCOME — time saved, revenue gained, errors reduced]

Requirements:
- Subject line: 3 options (result-focused, story-focused, curiosity)
- Tell a brief customer story (not a formal case study — more like a quick anecdote)
- Focus on the BEFORE and AFTER
- Connect their result to the reader's situation
- CTA: "Ready to get the same results? Here's your next step: [SPECIFIC ACTION]"
- 120-180 words
- Make it feel like a founder sharing a customer win, not a marketing email
```

> **Pro tip:** Day 2 is when motivation dips. Users who signed up with excitement yesterday now face the inertia of actually learning a new tool. A customer story re-ignites motivation by making the outcome feel achievable.

### Email 4: Feature Spotlight (Day 4)

```
Write a feature spotlight email for [PRODUCT NAME] sent on Day 4.

Context:
- Feature to highlight: [FEATURE NAME]
- What it does: [ONE-SENTENCE DESCRIPTION]
- Why users love it: [THE BENEFIT OR OUTCOME]
- How to access it: [WHERE TO FIND IT IN THE APP]
- Common use case: [A SPECIFIC SCENARIO WHERE THIS FEATURE SHINES]

Requirements:
- Subject line: 3 options (benefit-driven, did-you-know, use-case)
- Open with the problem this feature solves (not the feature itself)
- Show exactly how to use it in 2-3 steps
- Include a specific example: "For instance, if you're a [ROLE], you could use this to [SPECIFIC ACTION]"
- CTA: Link directly to the feature in the app
- 100-150 words
```

### Email 5: Social Proof & Community (Day 6)

```
Write a social proof email for [PRODUCT NAME] sent on Day 6.

Context:
- Community channel: [SLACK, DISCORD, FORUM, OR "NONE"]
- User count or proof point: [HOW MANY USERS/COMPANIES USE THE PRODUCT]
- Testimonial: [A QUOTE FROM A REAL OR REPRESENTATIVE USER]
- Community benefit: [WHAT THEY GET FROM JOINING — tips, templates, networking]

Requirements:
- Subject line: 3 options (community, social proof, FOMO)
- Lead with a trust-building metric ("Join [NUMBER]+ [PERSONAS] who...")
- Include 1-2 short testimonial quotes
- If you have a community: Invite them to join with a specific benefit
- If no community: Highlight that real teams/people rely on this daily
- CTA: Join community OR explore what others are building
- 100-150 words
```

### Email 6: Trial Reminder (Day 10 of 14-Day Trial)

```
Write a trial reminder email for [PRODUCT NAME] sent 4 days before trial ends.

Context:
- Trial end date: [DATE OR "in 4 days"]
- What they'll lose access to: [KEY FEATURES THAT REQUIRE PAID PLAN]
- What stays available: [FREE TIER FEATURES, IF ANY]
- Pricing: [STARTER PLAN PRICE]
- Upgrade link: [URL]
- Special offer: [DISCOUNT OR EXTENDED TRIAL FOR UPGRADING NOW, OR "NONE"]

Requirements:
- Subject line: 3 options (urgency without panic, value reminder, straightforward)
- Don't lead with "your trial is ending" — lead with value they'd lose
- Remind them of what they've accomplished during the trial (use a dynamic metric if possible: "[USER], you've created [X] projects so far")
- Clearly state what happens when the trial ends
- Present the upgrade as a natural next step, not a hard sell
- If there's a special offer, present it as a thank-you — not desperation
- CTA: Upgrade button
- 120-180 words
```

### Email 7: Trial Final Day (Day 13 of 14-Day Trial)

```
Write a last-day trial email for [PRODUCT NAME] sent on the final day of the trial.

Context:
- What happens tomorrow: [ACCOUNT DOWNGRADED / LOCKED / DATA RETAINED FOR X DAYS]
- Upgrade link: [URL]
- Pricing: [PLAN OPTIONS]
- Risk reversal: [MONEY-BACK GUARANTEE, CANCEL ANYTIME, ETC.]
- Data safety: [IS THEIR DATA SAFE IF THEY DON'T UPGRADE? FOR HOW LONG?]

Requirements:
- Subject line: 3 options (final day, don't lose progress, decision time)
- Be direct: "Your trial ends tomorrow"
- Reassure them about their data
- Summarize the value in one sentence
- Address the biggest purchase objection: [MOST COMMON REASON PEOPLE DON'T UPGRADE]
- Include risk reversal prominently
- One clear CTA button to upgrade
- 100-150 words
- Tone: Helpful, not desperate or aggressive
```

> **Why this works:** The final trial email should remove friction, not add pressure. Users who've made it to Day 13 already see value — they need reassurance that upgrading is low-risk, not more feature pitches.

## In-App Onboarding Copy

### First-Run Welcome Modal

```
Write copy for a first-run welcome modal in [PRODUCT NAME].

Context:
- Shown when: User logs in for the very first time
- Goal: Orient them and get them started on [KEY FIRST ACTION]
- Number of setup steps: [3-5 STEPS]

Write:
1. Modal headline (5-8 words, welcoming)
2. One-sentence subheadline setting expectations ("Takes about [X] minutes to set up")
3. A brief list of what they'll set up (3-5 items, one line each)
4. Primary CTA button: "Get Started" or similar
5. Secondary link: "Skip for now — I'll explore on my own"
6. Progress indicator text (e.g., "Step 1 of 4")

Keep total copy under 50 words (excluding the step list). Users won't read paragraphs in a modal.
```

### Empty State Copy (5 Variations)

```
Write empty state copy for [PRODUCT NAME].

Create copy for 5 different empty states:

1. [MAIN ENTITY — e.g., "Projects", "Dashboards", "Campaigns"] — the primary thing users create
2. [SECONDARY ENTITY — e.g., "Team members", "Integrations", "Reports"]
3. [DATA VIEW — e.g., "Analytics", "Activity feed", "Notifications"]
4. [SEARCH — no results found]
5. [INBOX/QUEUE — nothing to action]

For each empty state write:
- Illustration suggestion (describe the visual in one line)
- Headline (5-10 words — friendly, not robotic)
- Body text (1-2 sentences — explain what goes here and why it's valuable)
- CTA button text (action verb + object)

Requirements:
- Never say "Nothing here yet" or "No data found" — these are missed opportunities
- Frame each empty state as the BEGINNING of something exciting
- Make the CTA impossible to misunderstand
- Keep total copy per state under 30 words (headline + body + CTA)
```

> **Pro tip:** Empty states are one of the most underrated conversion tools in SaaS. Every new user sees them, and they either motivate the next action or create doubt. Great empty states feel like a helpful guide, not a dead end.

### Onboarding Tooltip Sequence

```
Write an onboarding tooltip sequence for [PRODUCT NAME].

Context:
- This is a guided product tour shown to new users
- Total tooltips: [5-7]
- Tool/feature being toured: [THE MAIN INTERFACE OR WORKFLOW]

For each tooltip in the sequence:
- What UI element it points to: [DESCRIBE THE ELEMENT]
- Tooltip headline (3-6 words)
- Tooltip body (1-2 sentences, max 25 words)
- Action button text (if applicable)
- "Skip tour" option text

Sequence order:
1. [FIRST ELEMENT — e.g., navigation bar, create button]
2. [SECOND ELEMENT]
3. [THIRD ELEMENT]
4. [FOURTH ELEMENT]
5. [FIFTH ELEMENT]
6. [FINAL ELEMENT — the key action]

Requirements:
- Each tooltip should answer "what is this?" and "why do I care?"
- Use progressive disclosure — don't explain everything at once
- Final tooltip should lead directly to the aha moment action
- Keep language conversational, not technical
- Include progress indicator (e.g., "3 of 6")
```

### Onboarding Checklist Copy

```
Write onboarding checklist copy for [PRODUCT NAME].

This is a persistent checklist shown in the app sidebar or dashboard until completed.

Checklist items (in recommended order):
1. [STEP 1 — e.g., "Complete your profile"]
2. [STEP 2 — e.g., "Create your first project"]
3. [STEP 3 — e.g., "Invite a teammate"]
4. [STEP 4 — e.g., "Connect an integration"]
5. [STEP 5 — e.g., "Set up your first automation"]

For each item write:
- Checklist label (3-6 words, starts with action verb)
- Help text shown on hover (one sentence explaining why this matters)
- Completion celebration text (shown when they check it off)

Also write:
- Checklist header text (e.g., "Get up and running")
- Progress text (e.g., "3 of 5 complete — almost there!")
- Completion celebration (shown when all items are done)
- Reward for completion: [WHAT THEY GET — badge, extended trial, template, nothing]
```

### Error State & Loading Copy

```
Write friendly error and loading state copy for [PRODUCT NAME].

Write copy for these scenarios:

1. Page loading:
   - Loading message (3-5 words)
   - Loading tip (one helpful fact or tip shown during loads)

2. Action in progress:
   - Saving: [CONFIRMATION TEXT]
   - Processing: [IN-PROGRESS TEXT]
   - Syncing: [SYNC TEXT]

3. Error states:
   - Generic error: Headline + body + CTA
   - Network error: Headline + body + CTA
   - Permission denied: Headline + body + CTA
   - Rate limited: Headline + body + CTA
   - Feature not available on plan: Headline + body + upgrade CTA

4. 404 page:
   - Headline
   - Body (1-2 sentences)
   - CTA button to go home
   - Optional: Suggest what they might be looking for

Requirements:
- Tone: [BRAND VOICE] — even errors should feel on-brand
- Never blame the user
- Always provide a clear next action
- Error copy should reduce anxiety, not create it
```

## Activation Milestone Emails

### First Key Action Completed

```
Write a celebration email sent when a user completes [KEY ACTIVATION ACTION — e.g., "created their first project"].

Context:
- What they just did: [THE ACTION]
- Why it matters: [HOW THIS UNLOCKS VALUE]
- What to do next: [THE LOGICAL NEXT STEP]
- Link to next step: [URL]

Requirements:
- Subject line: 3 options (celebration, progress, momentum)
- Congratulate them genuinely but briefly (one sentence)
- Show them what they unlocked by taking this action
- Point them to the next logical step
- Include a mini progress indicator if applicable ("You've completed 2 of 5 setup steps")
- CTA: The next action
- 80-120 words
- Tone: Encouraging coach, not cheerleader
```

### Team Invite Milestone

```
Write an email sent when a user invites their first teammate to [PRODUCT NAME].

Context:
- What just happened: They invited [a teammate/their team]
- Why collaboration matters: [HOW THE PRODUCT GETS BETTER WITH A TEAM]
- Next collaborative feature to try: [A FEATURE THAT'S BETTER WITH MULTIPLE USERS]

Requirements:
- Subject line: 3 options (team-focused, collaboration, growth)
- Acknowledge the invite is a big step (it means they see long-term value)
- Briefly explain how the product improves with team usage
- Suggest a collaborative action they can take together
- CTA: Link to the collaborative feature
- 80-120 words
```

### Usage Milestone Celebration

```
Write a milestone email for [PRODUCT NAME] users who hit a usage threshold.

Context:
- Milestone: [WHAT THEY ACHIEVED — e.g., "processed 1,000 records", "completed 50 tasks", "saved 10 hours"]
- Metric: [THE SPECIFIC NUMBER]
- Comparison: [HOW THIS COMPARES — "That puts you in the top 10% of users" OR SKIP]

Requirements:
- Subject line: 3 options (achievement, data-driven, celebration)
- Lead with the metric — make it feel impressive
- Put it in context (what it means, how it compares)
- Connect the milestone to the value they've received
- Soft CTA: Share their result, upgrade for more, or explore an advanced feature
- 80-120 words
- No heavy sell — this is a relationship-building moment
```

> **Why this works:** Milestone emails have 3x the engagement of regular marketing emails because they're about the USER, not your product. They reinforce the habit loop: action > reward > motivation > repeat.

### Integration Connected

```
Write an email sent when a user connects their first integration in [PRODUCT NAME].

Context:
- Integration connected: [INTEGRATION NAME — e.g., Slack, Salesforce, GitHub]
- What this unlocks: [THE VALUE OF THIS INTEGRATION]
- Suggested next integration: [ANOTHER POPULAR INTEGRATION]
- Setup guide link: [URL TO INTEGRATION DOCS]

Requirements:
- Subject line: 3 options (connected, unlocked, power-up)
- Confirm the integration is active
- Show them one specific thing they can now do that they couldn't before
- Suggest a power-user tip for this integration
- Soft-mention the next integration they should consider
- CTA: Try the integration feature or explore more integrations
- 80-120 words
```

## Trial-to-Paid Conversion Emails

### Value Summary Email (Mid-Trial)

```
Write a mid-trial value summary email for [PRODUCT NAME].

Context:
- Sent on: Day [X] of [TRIAL LENGTH]-day trial
- Goal: Show them the value they've already received
- Dynamic metrics to include (pick what applies):
  - [METRIC 1 — e.g., "You've created [X] projects"]
  - [METRIC 2 — e.g., "Your team has saved [X] hours"]
  - [METRIC 3 — e.g., "You've processed [X] records"]
- What they haven't tried yet: [A FEATURE THEY SHOULD EXPLORE]

Requirements:
- Subject line: 3 options (your progress, value recap, mid-trial check)
- Lead with their personal usage data — make it feel tailored, not mass-mailed
- Frame metrics as value received ("You've saved your team X hours" > "You've used X features")
- Gently surface a feature they haven't tried
- Don't mention upgrading — this is a value-reinforcement email
- CTA: Explore the untried feature
- 120-180 words
```

### Upgrade Nudge for Power Users

```
Write an upgrade nudge email for [PRODUCT NAME] users who are hitting free plan limits.

Context:
- What limit they're approaching: [SPECIFIC LIMIT — e.g., "5 of 5 projects used", "reaching 1,000 API calls"]
- What the paid plan unlocks: [KEY PAID FEATURES]
- Pricing: [PLAN NAME at PRICE/MONTH]
- Upgrade link: [URL]

Requirements:
- Subject line: 3 options (growth, limits, next level)
- Acknowledge they're getting real value (hitting limits is a good sign!)
- Frame the upgrade as removing a constraint on their growth, not a paywall
- Be specific about what they'll get: [LIST 3-4 PAID FEATURES]
- Include risk reversal: [GUARANTEE, CANCEL ANYTIME, ETC.]
- CTA: Upgrade button
- 120-180 words
- Tone: Helpful advisor, not pushy salesperson
```

> **Pro tip:** Users who hit plan limits are your highest-intent prospects. They've already proven the product is valuable. The upgrade email should feel like removing handcuffs, not asking for money.

### Objection-Handling Email Sequence

```
Write a 3-email objection-handling sequence for [PRODUCT NAME] trial users who haven't upgraded.

Common objections:
1. [OBJECTION 1 — e.g., "It's too expensive"]
2. [OBJECTION 2 — e.g., "I need to get team buy-in"]
3. [OBJECTION 3 — e.g., "I'm not sure it does enough"]

For each email:
- Subject line: 3 options
- Address the specific objection head-on
- Provide a counter-argument with evidence (data, testimonial, comparison)
- Include a relevant customer quote or case study reference
- CTA specific to the objection (ROI calculator, team pitch deck, feature comparison)
- 120-180 words per email
- Send timing: [DAYS AFTER TRIAL ENDS — e.g., Day 1, Day 4, Day 7]

Important:
- Never be dismissive of the objection
- Acknowledge it as valid, then reframe
- Each email should stand alone (they might only read one)
```

### Annual Plan Pitch (During Trial)

```
Write an email pitching the annual plan to [PRODUCT NAME] trial users.

Context:
- Monthly price: [MONTHLY PRICE]
- Annual price: [ANNUAL PRICE PER MONTH]
- Savings: [PERCENTAGE OR DOLLAR AMOUNT SAVED]
- Annual-only features or bonuses: [LIST ANY, OR "NONE"]

Requirements:
- Subject line: 3 options (savings, smart move, math-driven)
- Lead with the math: Show the exact dollar savings
- Frame it as a commitment to their own success, not just a discount
- If there are annual bonuses: Highlight them prominently
- Address the commitment fear: "Cancel anytime with a prorated refund" (if applicable)
- Include a comparison table: Monthly vs. Annual
- CTA: Choose annual plan
- 120-160 words
```

## Feature Adoption Nudges

### Unused Feature Discovery

```
Write a feature discovery email for [PRODUCT NAME] users who haven't tried [FEATURE NAME].

Context:
- Feature: [FEATURE NAME]
- What it does: [ONE-SENTENCE DESCRIPTION]
- Why it's valuable: [THE OUTCOME IT ENABLES]
- How users typically discover it: [WHERE IT LIVES IN THE APP]
- Time to value: [HOW QUICKLY THEY'LL SEE RESULTS — e.g., "See results in 5 minutes"]

Requirements:
- Subject line: 3 options (did you know, unlock, hidden gem)
- Don't say "You haven't tried this yet" — that feels like surveillance
- Instead, frame as "Here's something that might help with [PAIN POINT]"
- Show a quick 2-3 step how-to
- Include a specific use case example
- CTA: Link directly to the feature
- 100-150 words
```

### Power User Feature Highlight

```
Write an advanced feature email for [PRODUCT NAME] power users.

Context:
- Power user definition: [WHAT QUALIFIES THEM — e.g., daily active, 100+ records, team of 5+]
- Advanced feature: [FEATURE NAME]
- What it does: [DESCRIPTION]
- How it helps power users specifically: [ADVANCED USE CASE]
- Documentation link: [URL]

Requirements:
- Subject line: 3 options (level up, advanced, pro tip)
- Acknowledge they're a power user (ego stroke is earned — they use the product a lot)
- Introduce the advanced feature as a natural next step
- Show a specific workflow or automation they can build
- Include one "power user tip" that isn't in the docs
- CTA: Try it / Read the guide
- 120-160 words
```

### Mobile App / Extension Nudge

```
Write an email nudging [PRODUCT NAME] users to install [MOBILE APP / BROWSER EXTENSION / DESKTOP APP].

Context:
- What they should install: [APP NAME AND PLATFORM]
- Key benefit of the additional platform: [WHAT IT ENABLES THAT WEB DOESN'T]
- Download link: [URL]
- Current usage: They're active on [WEB / DESKTOP / MOBILE]

Requirements:
- Subject line: 3 options (convenience, speed, access)
- Lead with a scenario where the extra platform saves them time
- 2-3 specific things they can do on [PLATFORM] that are faster/better
- Don't make it sound like web is insufficient — frame as "and also"
- CTA: Download / Install button
- 80-120 words
```

### New Feature Announcement (In-App + Email)

```
Write a new feature announcement for [PRODUCT NAME] in two formats.

Feature: [FEATURE NAME]
What it does: [DESCRIPTION]
Why you built it: [USER REQUEST, PAIN POINT IT SOLVES, COMPETITIVE GAP]
How to access it: [WHERE IN THE APP]
Who benefits most: [WHICH USER SEGMENT]

Format 1 — In-App Banner:
- Banner headline (5-8 words)
- Banner body (one sentence, under 20 words)
- CTA button text
- Dismiss text

Format 2 — Email Announcement:
- Subject line: 3 options
- Opening that connects to the user pain this solves
- What's new in 2-3 sentences
- How to use it in 3 quick steps
- CTA: Try it now
- 120-160 words

Format 3 — Changelog Entry:
- Title
- Category tag (New Feature / Improvement / Fix)
- 3-5 sentence description
- Screenshot description suggestion
```

## Onboarding Optimization Prompts

### Onboarding Flow Audit

```
Audit my current onboarding flow for [PRODUCT NAME] and suggest improvements.

Current flow:
1. [STEP 1 — e.g., "User signs up with email"]
2. [STEP 2 — e.g., "Sees welcome screen with 3 options"]
3. [STEP 3 — e.g., "Creates workspace"]
4. [STEP 4 — e.g., "Invites team (optional)"]
5. [STEP 5 — e.g., "Lands on empty dashboard"]
6. [STEP 6 — e.g., "Has to figure out what to do"]

Current metrics:
- Signup to first action: [PERCENTAGE]
- Day 1 retention: [PERCENTAGE]
- Day 7 retention: [PERCENTAGE]
- Trial to paid conversion: [PERCENTAGE]

Analyze:
- Where are users most likely dropping off and why?
- Which steps create friction vs. value?
- What's missing from this flow?
- Suggest a revised flow with specific copy for each step
- Recommend 3 quick wins I can implement this week
- Recommend 3 bigger structural changes for next quarter
```

### Activation Metric Identifier

```
Help me identify the right activation metric for [PRODUCT NAME].

Product: [WHAT IT DOES]
User actions available to track:
1. [ACTION 1 — e.g., "Created a project"]
2. [ACTION 2 — e.g., "Invited a team member"]
3. [ACTION 3 — e.g., "Connected an integration"]
4. [ACTION 4 — e.g., "Completed a workflow"]
5. [ACTION 5 — e.g., "Shared a report"]
6. [ACTION 6 — e.g., "Used the product 3 days in a row"]

Current retention curve: [DESCRIBE — e.g., "Big drop on Day 2, stabilizes around Day 14"]

Help me figure out:
- Which of these actions is most likely the "aha moment"?
- What combination of actions predicts long-term retention?
- What timeframe should I measure activation in? (Day 1, Day 3, Day 7?)
- What's a realistic activation rate target for my stage?
- How should I design the onboarding to drive this specific action?
```

> **Why this works:** Most SaaS companies either don't have an activation metric or track the wrong one. The right activation metric is the action most correlated with long-term retention — not the action most users take. Finding it changes everything about how you design onboarding.

### Segmented Onboarding Paths

```
Design segmented onboarding paths for [PRODUCT NAME].

User segments:
1. [SEGMENT 1 — e.g., "Solo freelancers"]
2. [SEGMENT 2 — e.g., "Small team leads (2-10 people)"]
3. [SEGMENT 3 — e.g., "Enterprise admins"]

For each segment, design:
- How to identify them (what question to ask during signup)
- Their unique "aha moment" (what activation looks like for them)
- First action they should take (different per segment)
- Welcome email variation
- Onboarding checklist items (3-5, customized)
- Feature highlights that matter most to them
- Trial-to-paid messaging angle

Also suggest:
- The segmentation question format (radio buttons, dropdown, cards)
- Copy for the segmentation step
- What to do if someone doesn't match a segment cleanly
```
