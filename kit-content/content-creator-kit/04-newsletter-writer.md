## How to Use This File

Each section contains a complete newsletter template with the AI prompt, variable slots, and formatting guidance. These templates work with any email platform: Substack, Beehiiv, ConvertKit, Mailchimp, Ghost, and others.

**Before using any template,** paste this brand context into your AI session:

```
NEWSLETTER CONTEXT:
- Newsletter name: [YOUR NEWSLETTER NAME]
- Niche/topic: [WHAT YOU WRITE ABOUT]
- Audience: [WHO READS YOUR NEWSLETTER]
- Frequency: [HOW OFTEN YOU SEND]
- Tone: [YOUR WRITING VOICE]
- Typical length: [WORD COUNT RANGE]
- Primary goal: [e.g., educate, entertain, drive product sales, build community]
- CTA focus: [WHAT YOU USUALLY ASK READERS TO DO]
```

## Template 1: Weekly Roundup Newsletter

**Best for:** Curated insights, industry updates, or personal highlights from the week.
**Ideal frequency:** Weekly
**Reader expectation:** "Catch me up on what I missed."

### Prompt

```
Write a weekly roundup newsletter for my audience of [AUDIENCE] about [NICHE/TOPIC].

THIS WEEK'S CONTENT:
- Topic 1: [BRIEFLY DESCRIBE — e.g., "A new study on remote work productivity"]
- Topic 2: [BRIEFLY DESCRIBE — e.g., "My latest blog post about content repurposing"]
- Topic 3: [BRIEFLY DESCRIBE — e.g., "A tool I discovered this week"]
- Topic 4: [BRIEFLY DESCRIBE — e.g., "A tweet/post that made me think"]
- Topic 5: [BRIEFLY DESCRIBE — e.g., "An upcoming event or deadline"] (optional)

PERSONAL NOTE: [A brief personal update, thought, or anecdote from your week — 1-2 sentences is enough]

NEWSLETTER STRUCTURE:

SUBJECT LINE: Write 3 options (I'll pick one). Rules:
- Under 50 characters
- Create curiosity or promise a benefit
- Don't use the newsletter name in the subject line
- Avoid spam trigger words (FREE, URGENT, ACT NOW)

PREVIEW TEXT: Write 1 option. Rules:
- Under 90 characters
- Complements the subject line (don't repeat it)
- Creates additional reason to open

OPENING (2-3 sentences):
Start with a casual, personal greeting. Share one sentence about your week or a thought that connects to the newsletter theme. DO NOT say "Welcome to this week's edition of..." — that's boring.

SECTION 1-5 (one per topic above):
For each topic, write:
- A bold section header (catchy, not generic)
- 2-4 sentences: What it is, why it matters, and your take on it
- A link prompt: [Link: description] (I'll insert the actual URL)
- Keep each section scannable — bold key phrases, use short paragraphs

SIGN-OFF:
- A quick personal note or question for readers
- A clear CTA: "Reply and tell me..." or "Share this with someone who..."
- Your name and a brief sign-off line

FORMATTING RULES:
- Total length: 400-600 words
- Use bold headers for each section
- Use line breaks liberally — emails are read on phones
- Include at least one "hot take" or personal opinion (not just summaries)
- Write like you're emailing a friend, not publishing an article

TONE: [YOUR TONE]
```

## Template 2: Deep Dive / Essay Newsletter

**Best for:** Thought leadership, long-form analysis, teaching a concept in depth.
**Ideal frequency:** Weekly or biweekly
**Reader expectation:** "Teach me something I can't learn from a quick Google search."

### Prompt

```
Write a deep-dive newsletter essay about [TOPIC].

ESSAY DETAILS:
- Main argument or thesis: [YOUR CORE POINT IN ONE SENTENCE]
- Key supporting points:
  1. [POINT 1]
  2. [POINT 2]
  3. [POINT 3]
- Examples or case studies to reference: [LIST ANY SPECIFIC EXAMPLES]
- Counterargument to address: [WHAT SOMEONE WHO DISAGREES MIGHT SAY]
- Actionable takeaway: [WHAT THE READER SHOULD DO DIFFERENTLY AFTER READING]

SUBJECT LINE: Write 3 options that create a curiosity gap or make a bold claim. Rules:
- Under 50 characters
- The reader should think "I NEED to read this"
- Formats that work: questions, bold statements, "[Topic]: what nobody tells you"

PREVIEW TEXT: Write 1 option that adds context without giving away the punchline.

ESSAY STRUCTURE:

OPENING HOOK (1-2 paragraphs):
Start with one of these:
- A surprising fact or statistic
- A short personal story (under 50 words)
- A bold, provocative statement
- A question the reader has been asking themselves
The hook should make the reader forget they were about to close the email.

THE SETUP (1-2 paragraphs):
Frame the problem or context. Why does this topic matter right now? Why should the reader care? Set up the tension that your essay will resolve.

THE MEAT (3-5 paragraphs):
This is where you deliver. For each supporting point:
- State the point clearly in one sentence
- Explain it with evidence, examples, or personal experience
- Connect it back to the main thesis
Use subheadings to break up long sections.

THE COUNTERARGUMENT (1 paragraph):
Address the strongest objection. "Now, some people would argue..." Then explain why your position still holds. This builds massive credibility.

THE TAKEAWAY (1-2 paragraphs):
Distill everything into a clear, actionable conclusion. What should the reader do, think, or believe differently?

CLOSING:
A memorable final line — something quotable that sticks. Then a CTA: invite replies, ask a question, or point to a related resource.

FORMATTING RULES:
- Total length: 800-1,200 words
- Use subheadings every 200-300 words
- Bold key sentences (the ones a skimmer should catch)
- One idea per paragraph
- Short paragraphs (2-3 sentences max)
- Write in second person ("you") to keep it personal

TONE: [YOUR TONE — e.g., intellectual but accessible / passionate and opinionated / calm and analytical]
```

## Template 3: Curated Links Newsletter

**Best for:** Sharing valuable resources, articles, tools, and finds from around the internet.
**Ideal frequency:** Weekly or daily
**Reader expectation:** "Show me the best stuff I would've missed."

### Prompt

```
Write a curated links newsletter featuring these items:

LINKS TO INCLUDE:
1. [TITLE/DESCRIPTION OF LINK 1] — [URL or "I'll add URL later"]
   My take: [1 SENTENCE — why this matters]
2. [TITLE/DESCRIPTION OF LINK 2] — [URL or "I'll add URL later"]
   My take: [1 SENTENCE]
3. [TITLE/DESCRIPTION OF LINK 3] — [URL or "I'll add URL later"]
   My take: [1 SENTENCE]
4. [TITLE/DESCRIPTION OF LINK 4] — [URL or "I'll add URL later"]
   My take: [1 SENTENCE]
5. [TITLE/DESCRIPTION OF LINK 5] — [URL or "I'll add URL later"]
   My take: [1 SENTENCE]
(Add more as needed — 5-10 links is ideal)

CATEGORIZE INTO:
- [CATEGORY 1 — e.g., "Must Read", "Tools", "Trending"]
- [CATEGORY 2 — e.g., "Deep Dives", "Quick Hits", "For Fun"]
- [CATEGORY 3 — optional]

SUBJECT LINE: Write 3 options. Formats that work for curated newsletters:
- "5 things worth your time this week"
- "[Best find] + [number] more"
- Reference the most interesting link in the subject

PREVIEW TEXT: Tease the top 1-2 links.

NEWSLETTER STRUCTURE:

INTRO (2-3 sentences):
A quick personal note or observation that sets the theme. If there's no theme, a brief "what I'm thinking about" hook works.

FEATURED LINK:
Give the best link the most real estate. Write 3-5 sentences about why it matters and what the reader will get from it.

REMAINING LINKS (organized by category):
For each link:
- **Bold title** — [Link placeholder]
- 1-2 sentence description with my editorial take (not just a summary — add an OPINION)
- Use a consistent emoji or bullet style for visual rhythm

BONUS SECTION (optional):
"One thing I made this week" or "A tweet that made me think" — a personal touch.

SIGN-OFF:
Quick closing + CTA to share or reply.

FORMATTING RULES:
- Total length: 300-500 words
- Each link description should be 1-3 sentences MAX — keep it tight
- Use bold headers for categories
- The value is your CURATION and COMMENTARY, not the links themselves

TONE: [YOUR TONE]
```

## Template 4: Product Launch Announcement

**Best for:** Launching a new product, course, template, or offering to your email list.
**Ideal frequency:** As needed
**Reader expectation:** Depends on your relationship — the email needs to earn the right to sell.

### Prompt

```
Write a product launch email for my newsletter audience.

PRODUCT DETAILS:
- Product name: [NAME]
- What it is: [1 SENTENCE DESCRIPTION]
- Price: [PRICE — include any launch discount]
- Who it's for: [TARGET CUSTOMER]
- Key benefits (not features):
  1. [BENEFIT 1 — what it helps them DO or ACHIEVE]
  2. [BENEFIT 2]
  3. [BENEFIT 3]
- What makes it different: [YOUR UNIQUE ANGLE]
- Link to buy: [URL or "I'll add later"]
- Launch timeline: [e.g., "Available now" or "Doors open Tuesday" or "50% off for 48 hours"]
- Social proof (if any): [TESTIMONIALS, BETA USER RESULTS, NUMBER OF USERS]

SUBJECT LINE: Write 5 options across these styles:
1. Curiosity-driven (doesn't mention the product directly)
2. Benefit-driven (leads with the outcome)
3. Urgency-driven (time-limited element)
4. Social proof-driven (references results or testimonials)
5. Direct announcement ("I made something new")

PREVIEW TEXT: Write 2 options that complement the best subject line.

EMAIL STRUCTURE:

OPENING (2-3 paragraphs):
Do NOT start with "I'm excited to announce..." — that's about you, not the reader.
Instead, start with the PROBLEM. Describe the pain, frustration, or unmet need that your audience feels. Make them nod and think "that's exactly my situation."

THE BRIDGE (1-2 paragraphs):
Transition from the problem to your solution. Share the story of WHY you built this. What was missing from existing solutions? Keep it brief and genuine.

THE PRODUCT (2-3 paragraphs):
Introduce the product. Lead with benefits, not features.
Format benefits as: "You'll be able to [OUTCOME]" or "[PRODUCT] gives you [RESULT]"
Include 3-5 specific benefits in a bulleted list.

SOCIAL PROOF (1 paragraph):
If you have testimonials, beta user feedback, or early results, include them here. Format as quotes with names. If no social proof yet, skip this section.

THE OFFER (1 paragraph):
State the price, any launch discount, and what's included. Be transparent.
If there's urgency (limited time, limited spots), state it clearly.

CTA:
One clear button/link with action-oriented text: "Get [Product Name]" or "Start [Outcome] Today"
Repeat the CTA at least twice in the email (after the product section and at the end).

OBJECTION HANDLING (optional — 1 paragraph):
Address the #1 reason someone might hesitate. "Not sure if this is for you? Here's who it works best for..."

CLOSING:
Brief personal note. Remind them of the deadline/urgency if applicable. Sign off warmly.

P.S. LINE:
Always include a P.S. — it's the second most-read part of any email after the subject line. Use it to restate the key benefit or the deadline.

FORMATTING RULES:
- Total length: 400-700 words
- Short paragraphs (2-3 sentences)
- Bold the key benefit and the price
- Use bullet points for feature/benefit lists
- The email should feel like a recommendation from a friend, not a sales page
- Write in "you" language (80% about the reader, 20% about the product)

TONE: Enthusiastic but not hype-y. Confident but not pushy. Like recommending a restaurant you genuinely love.
```

## Template 5: Re-Engagement Email

**Best for:** Winning back subscribers who haven't opened emails recently or reactivating a dormant list.
**Ideal frequency:** Quarterly or as needed
**Reader expectation:** They've forgotten about you — this email needs to remind them why they subscribed.

### Prompt

```
Write a re-engagement email for subscribers who haven't opened my newsletter in [30/60/90] days.

CONTEXT:
- Newsletter name: [NAME]
- What my newsletter covers: [TOPIC]
- Best content from the past [30/60/90] days: [LIST 2-3 TOP PIECES]
- New developments: [ANYTHING NEW — new content format, new topics, improvements]
- Incentive (optional): [e.g., "Free resource for returning readers" or "none"]

SUBJECT LINE: Write 5 options designed to get OPENS from disengaged subscribers. Styles:
1. Personal check-in ("Quick question for you")
2. FOMO ("You missed this")
3. Direct and honest ("Should I stop emailing you?")
4. Value-forward ("The [topic] resource I wish I had")
5. Curiosity ("I noticed something about your account")

PREVIEW TEXT: Write 2 options that create urgency to open.

EMAIL STRUCTURE:

OPENING (1-2 paragraphs):
Be direct and human. Acknowledge that you haven't heard from them. Don't be guilt-trippy or passive-aggressive. Good energy: "Hey, I noticed you haven't opened these in a while. No judgment — life is busy."

REMINDER OF VALUE (1-2 paragraphs):
Remind them what they're getting. Not "we send weekly emails about X" but "You originally subscribed because [reason]. Here's what you've missed that might matter to you:"
List your 2-3 best recent pieces with brief descriptions.

THE ASK (1 paragraph):
Give them two clear options:
1. Stay subscribed — "If you want to keep getting these, click here [or just open the next email]"
2. Unsubscribe — "If not, no hard feelings. You can unsubscribe here."
Being willing to let them go actually makes more people stay.

INCENTIVE (optional — 1 paragraph):
If you have a freebie, discount, or exclusive piece of content, offer it here. "As a 'welcome back' — here's [free resource] on me."

CLOSING:
Short, warm, human. One sentence. Your name.

FORMATTING RULES:
- Total length: 200-350 words (short — they're already disengaged, don't overwhelm them)
- Ultra-short paragraphs (1-2 sentences each)
- One or two links maximum
- No images or complex formatting — keep it plain-text feeling
- The tone should feel like a DM from a real person, not a marketing email

TONE: Genuine, low-pressure, human. Not desperate.
```

## Subject Line Generator

Use this prompt anytime you need subject lines for any email.

### Universal Subject Line Generator

```
Generate 15 email subject lines for a newsletter about [TOPIC/THIS SPECIFIC EMAIL].

CONTEXT:
- Newsletter: [NAME]
- Audience: [WHO READS THIS]
- This email is about: [BRIEF SUMMARY OF THE EMAIL CONTENT]
- Goal: [OPEN RATE / CLICKS / REPLIES]

GENERATE 3 SUBJECT LINES FOR EACH CATEGORY:

1. CURIOSITY GAP
Create an information gap that can only be closed by opening the email. Don't reveal the answer.
(e.g., "The one thing I'd never do again", "This changed how I think about [topic]")

2. BENEFIT-DRIVEN
Promise a specific outcome or value.
(e.g., "How to [achieve result] in [timeframe]", "The [tool/method] that saved me [X hours]")

3. SOCIAL PROOF / NUMBERS
Use data, results, or other people's experiences.
(e.g., "How [person] grew to [number]", "I tested 7 methods. Here's what won.")

4. URGENCY / TIMELINESS
Create a reason to open NOW, not later.
(e.g., "Ends tonight: [offer]", "[Topic] is changing — here's what you need to know")

5. PERSONAL / CONVERSATIONAL
Write it like a text from a friend.
(e.g., "Quick question", "I need your opinion on something", "Honestly?")

RULES FOR ALL SUBJECT LINES:
- Under 50 characters (under 35 is ideal for mobile)
- No ALL CAPS
- No exclamation marks (unless truly warranted)
- No spam trigger words (FREE, GUARANTEED, ACT NOW)
- Lowercase is fine and often performs better
- Don't use the newsletter name in the subject line
- Each subject line should be meaningfully different — not just variations of the same idea
```

### Subject Line A/B Test Prompt

```
I need to A/B test subject lines for this email:

EMAIL TOPIC: [WHAT THE EMAIL IS ABOUT]
AUDIENCE: [YOUR AUDIENCE]

Write 2 subject lines that take OPPOSITE approaches:

VERSION A: Emotional / curiosity-based approach
VERSION B: Rational / benefit-based approach

For each, explain:
- The psychological trigger it uses
- Who it will appeal to most
- Predicted open rate comparison (which will likely win and why)

Then write a VERSION C: A hybrid that combines the best elements of both.
```

## Newsletter Writing Tips

- **Write the subject line LAST.** Write the email first, then create a subject line for the best part of the content.
- **Your first line is your second subject line.** Many email clients show the opening line as preview text. Make it count.
- **Write for skimmers.** Bold your key points. Use headers. Short paragraphs. Most people scan before deciding to read.
- **End every email with a question.** Replies boost deliverability and build relationships.
- **Send at consistent times.** Train your audience to expect you. Wednesday and Thursday mornings typically perform best, but test your own audience.
